We further conclude that the respondents have not established that their proposed group is
socially distinct in Ecuador. Unlike Gashi, where the respondent's "identity as one who had
cooperated with investigators of Haradinaj was well known in the society' and "[olther persons
who had cooperated with the investigation of war crimes were similarly targeted," 702 F.3d at 137,
here the respondent has not presented any evidence that Ecuadoran society as a whole perceives
"witnesses of gang crime who report to the police"' as a distinct group within the society. See.
Matter of W/-G-R-, 26 I&N Dec.. 208, 217 (BIA 2014) (*To have *social distinction' necessary to
establish a particular social group, there must be evidence'showing that society in general
perceives, considers, or recognizes persons sharing the particular characteristic to be a group."),
vacatedin part on other grounds by Reyes y. Lynch, 842 F.3d 1125 (9th Cir. 2016). Thus, although
the Immigration Judge did not address social distinction below, we need not remand for additional
fact-finding because the record evidence would not permit a factfinder to permissbly conclude
that the group is perceived as distinct. See Pullman-Standard v. Swint, 456 U.S. 273, 292 (1982)
([R]emand is the proper course unless the record permits only one resolution of the factual
issue."); Marvel Characters, Inc. v. Kirby, 726 F.3d 119, 132 (2d Cir. 2013) ("[W]here only one
determination by the district court would be within its discretion---there is no reason to remand.
If we did and the court decided to the contrary, we would be duty bound to reverse in any
event ...." (internal citation omitted); Reynolds v. Giuliani, 506 F.3d 183, 197 (2d Cir. 2007)
("[I]f the evidence before us admits only a single resolution of the controling issue, remanding
the case serves no useful purpose.'); In re Platinum and Palladium Antitrust Litig.,
XX XX , at *51 n.26 (S.D.N.Y.xx ,2017) (explaining that
the factfinder chooses between plausible alternatives, but differentiating plausible from
unsupported claims is a question of law), reconsidered by 449 F. Supp. 3d 290 (S.D.N.Y. 2020),
aff'd in part, vacated in part, rev'd in part by 61 F.4th 242 (2d Cir. 2023).
Because the respondents have not demonstrated that they are members of a cognizable
particular social group, we will deny ther applications for asylum and the lead respondent's
application for withholding of removal.
Turning to the lead respondent's application for protection under the CAT, we agree with the
Immigration Judge that the respondent has not demonstrated that he will more likely than not be
tortured in Ecuador by, at the instigation of, or with the consent or acquiescence ofa public official
(IJ at 11-12). See 8 C.F.R. §§ 1208.16(c), 1208.18(a). Although the respondent suffered some
physical harm by gang members in Ecuador in the past, he did not require medical treatment and
did not suffer any serious injuries (IJ at 11 n.4; Tr. at 40-41, 46, 48). See Ayv. Holder, 743 F.3d
317, 322 (2d Cir. 2014) (holding that a respondent who was *kicked, punched, and slapped after
being arrested by ... security forces," but did not require medical treatment did not suffer torture).
Overall, the respondent has not demonstrated .that his past harm was suficiently severe to rise to
the level of torture. See 8 C.F.R. § 1208.18(a)(2) (describing torture as an "extreme form of cruel
and inhuman treatment [that] does not include lesser forms of cruel. inhuman or degrading
treatment or punishment."); see also Garcia-Aranda v. Garland, 53 F.4th 752, 759 (2d Cir. 2022)
(describing torture as an "act by which severe pain or suffering, whether physical or mental, is
intentionally inflicted" (quoting 8 C.F.R. § 1208.18(a)(1))
