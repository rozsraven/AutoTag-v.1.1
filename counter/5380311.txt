We review the findings of fact, including the determination of credibility, made by the
Immigration Judge under the "clearly erroneous" standard. 8 C.F.R. § 1003.1(d)(3)(). We review
all other issues, including issues of law, discretion, or judgment, under a de novo standard.
8 C.F.R. § 1003.1(d)(3)(ii).
The Immigration Judge correctly determined that the respondent has not suffered past
persecution and does not have a well-founded fear of future persecution on account of his actual
or imputed political opinion (IJ at 8-10; Respondents' Br. at 13-16). "[TJo qualify as a politica1
opinion,' an opinion must involve some support for or disagreement with the belief system,
policies, or practices of a government or its instrumentalities; an entity that seeks to directly
influence laws, regulations, or policy; an organization that aims to overthrow the government; or
a group that plays some other similar role in society. Zelaya-Moreno v. Wilkinson, 989 F.3d 190.
199-200 (2d Cir. 2021) (citations omitted). While the respondent testified that allowing the gangs
to engage in illegal conduct would have been against his morals (Tr. at 49), opposing the illegal
activities of gang members does not necessarily constitute a political opnion. See id. at 201
("[D]isapproving of things that have a negative impact on one's life or even one's country does
not necessarly amount to a political opinion'). The respondent did not testify that his
disagreement with the gangs is 'rooted in any sort of disagreement with the policies they seek to
impose nor any ideology they espouse." Id. at 203.
The respondents emphasize on appeal that the lead respondent testified that he wanted to fight
against the corruption of police oficers who cooperate with gangs (Tr. at 49; Respondents' Br.
at 15). However, the respondent did not claim to have expressed his opposition to corruption to
either the gang members that harmed him or any police oficers. Overall, this evidence does not
establish persecution on account of a political opinion. See id. at 198-99, 203 (differentiating
between instances m which opposition to govemment corruption may constitute a political opinion
and instances in which it will not)..
The respondents have also not demonstrated that the gang members who harmed the lead
respondent did so on account of an imputed political opinion. The Immigration Judge did not
clearly err in fnding that the respondent's attackers were not motivated by their belief that the
respondent held any particular political opinion (IJ at 9). See Matter of N-M-, 25 I&N Dec.. 526,
532 (BIA 2011) (holding that the motive of the persecutor is a factual finding the Board reviews
for clear error).. The gang members attacked the respondent for interfering with their ilegal.
activities and accused him of being an informant for calling the police (IJ at 8; Tr. at 34-35, 40,
46, 48). However, this does not demonstrate that the gang members perceived the respondent's
opposition as political: *[O]pposition to crimmal elements such as. gangs, even when such.
opposition incurs the enmity of these elements, does not thereby become political opposition
simply by virtue of the gang's reaction." See id. at 202. Likewise, the record evidence that gang
members have killed other security guards does not support the respondent's claim that the harm
he suffered was based on an inputed political opinion (Exh. 4, Tab L).
The respondents further argue that the gang members harmed the lead respondent on
account of his membership in a cognizable particular social group (Respondents' Br. at 8-13).

security guards, "Ecuadoran security guards who report gang members," and "witnesses of gang
crime who report to the police." We agree with the Immigration Judge that the proposed particular
social group of 'former Ecuadoran security guards" is not socially distinct in Ecuador (IJ at 7).
A proposed group is socially distinct if "the people of a given society would perceive [it] as
sufficiently separate or distinct." Quintanilla-Mejia v. Garland, 3 F.4th 569, 588 (2d Cir. 2021)
(citing Matter ofM-E-V-G-, 26 I&N Dec.. 227, 241 (BIA 2014)). The Immigration Judge did not
clearly err in finding that the record evidence-including the articles regarding the killing of.
security guards-- does not demonstrate that Ecuadorian society perceives former security guards a
distimct group (IJ at 7; Exh. 4, Tab L). Although the respondent's testified that he took courses to
become a security guard and that security guards have certain responsibilities as part of their
employment, he has not demonstrated that former security guards have specialized skills that set
them apart from the general population in Ecuador (Tr. at 43, 46-47, 59). Cf. Plancarte Sauceda
v. Garland, 23 F.4th 824, 834 (9th Cir. 2022) (holding that nurses are a cognizable particular social
group because they have received medical training and have specialized nursing skills that are
not shared by the general population and which they retan even if they leave the profession).
Thus, this does not constitute a cognizable particular social group.
The Immigration Judge also found the proposed particular social group of'Ecuadoran security
guards who report gang members" not cognizable because it was not based on an immutable
characteristic (IJ at 7). An immutable characteristic is one that "cannot change or should not
be required to change because it is fundamental to [the group members'] individual identities
or consciences." Quintanilla-Mejia, 3 F.4th at 588. The Immigration Judge correctly concluded
that one's occupation as a security guard is not an immutable characteristic that either cannot
change or should not be required to change (IJ at 7). See Matter of Acosta, 19 I&N Dec.. 211,
234 (BIA 1985), overruled in part on other grounds by Matter of Mogharrabi, 19 I&N Dec.. 439
(BIA 1987).
The respondents argue on appeal that this proposed group is immutable because it is based
on a shared past experience of having reported gang members (Respondents' Br. at 10). See
Koudriachova v. Gonzales, 490 F.3d 255, 263 (2d Cr. 2007). The respondents, however, have
not framed this group as being based on apast experience, but rather on present actions. The group
is composed of 'Ecuadorian security guards," not former security guards, who "report' gang
members, not "who have reported" gang members. Thus, we are not persuaded that the
respondents' proposed group is based on an immutable characteristic.
We aso conclude, upon de novo review, that this proposed particular social group does
not satisfy the-particularity requrement. See Matter of W-Y-C- & H-O-B-, 27 I&N Dec.. 189, 191
(BIA 2018) ([W]e review the ultimate determination whether a proposed group is cognizable
de novo ... .). A proposed group "must be defmed by characteristics that provide a clear
benchmark for determining who falls within the group." MatterofM-E-V-G-, 26 I&N Dec.. at 239.
The respondents' proposed group does not satisfy this requirement. The phrase "who report"
is vague because it does not identify to whom the report must be made. The proposed group could
include security guards who report gang members to the police, but it could also include security
guards who report gang members to their supervisors in their private security firms, or even those
who simply report gang activity to their spouses or other members of ther family. The phrase
3

is also vague because it is not clear whether reporting requires a formalized process or whether
simply communicating about gang members would satisfy the requirement. See id. (It is critical
that the terms used to describe the group have commonly accepted definitions ...'). Thus,
we conclude that the proposed group of 'Ecuadorian security guards who report gang members"
is not a cognizable particular social group.
The respondents also challenge the Immigration Judge's determination that the proposed group
of"witnesses of gang crime who report to the police"' is not cognizable (IJ-at 8; Respondents' Br.
at 11-13). The Immigration Judge's analysis focuses on whether the respondent's actions of
reporting the gangs to the police would render him a member of the particular social group of
"cooperating witnesses" recognized in Gashi v. Holder, 702 F.3d 130, 136-37 (2d Cir. 2012)
(IJ at 8). This analysis is flawed in several respects. First, whether the respondents are members
of a proposed particular social group is a separate consideration from whether the proposed group
is cognizable: See id. Second, the Immigration Judge did not analyze whether the specific
proposed group identified by the respondents satisfied the immutability, particularity, and social
distinction requirements for a cognizable group. See Matter of W-y-C- & H-O-B-, 27 I&N
Dec. at 191-92 (emphasizing that arespondent must clearly indicate the proposed particular social
group to which he or she claims to belong, and the Immigration Judge should analyze this specific
group). Third, Gashi involved a specific factual scenario involving a respondent who publicly
testified before an international tribunal, whose name appeared on apublic witness list, 'and whose
cooperation in a war crimes investigation was widely known within Kosovar society. See Gashi,
702 F.3d at 137. It did not establish that "cooperating witnesses" is broadly cognizable as amatter
of law outside of the specific factual scenario present in that case.
Upon our de novo review, we conclude that the respondents' proposed group of "witnesses of
gang crime who report to the police' is not cognizable. The Board has previously held that
cooperation with law enforcement may satisfy the requirements for a particular social group
"if the cooperation is public in nature, particularly where testimony was given in court
proceedings, and the evidence in the record reflects that the society in question recognzes and
provides protection for such cooperation." Matter of H-L-S-A-, 28 I&N Dec.. 228, 237 (BIA 2021).
The respondents' proposed group broadly includes all witnesses to gang crime who report to the
police, regardless of whether they do so in a public manner. This contrasts with Gashi, 702 F.3d
at 136-37, where the witnesses' testified publicly and their names appeared-on a publicly available
witness list, which bounded the group at issue. Additionally, the term "gang" is amorphous.
Unlike in Gashi, 702 F.3d at 136-37, where the particular social group was based on being
a witness against a certain individual, here the proposed group encompasses witnesses to 'gang'
crime generally, without specifying a particular gang or defining what constitutes a"gang." The
affidavits the respondents submit in support of their claim us different terms to refer to the
individuals the respondent fears, including "gangs," "criminals," and "micro-traffickers" (Exh. 4,
Tab K). Because the respondents have not demonstrated that the group of "witnesses of gang
crime who report to the police' has clear benchmarks has clear benchmarks for who qualifies
as a witness and that the term "gang' has a commonly accepted defnition in Ecuador, it cannot
satisfy the particularity requirement for a cognizable group. See Matter of M-E-V/-G-, 26 I&N
Dec. at 239.

We further conclude that the respondents have not established that their proposed group is
socially distinct in Ecuador. Unlike Gashi, where the respondent's "identity as one who had
cooperated with investigators of Haradinaj was well known in the society' and "[olther persons
who had cooperated with the investigation of war crimes were similarly targeted," 702 F.3d at 137,
here the respondent has not presented any evidence that Ecuadoran society as a whole perceives
"witnesses of gang crime who report to the police"' as a distinct group within the society. See.
Matter of W/-G-R-, 26 I&N Dec.. 208, 217 (BIA 2014) (*To have *social distinction' necessary to
establish a particular social group, there must be evidence'showing that society in general
perceives, considers, or recognizes persons sharing the particular characteristic to be a group."),
vacatedin part on other grounds by Reyes y. Lynch, 842 F.3d 1125 (9th Cir. 2016). Thus, although
the Immigration Judge did not address social distinction below, we need not remand for additional
fact-finding because the record evidence would not permit a factfinder to permissbly conclude
that the group is perceived as distinct. See Pullman-Standard v. Swint, 456 U.S. 273, 292 (1982)
([R]emand is the proper course unless the record permits only one resolution of the factual
issue."); Marvel Characters, Inc. v. Kirby, 726 F.3d 119, 132 (2d Cir. 2013) ("[W]here only one
determination by the district court would be within its discretion---there is no reason to remand.
If we did and the court decided to the contrary, we would be duty bound to reverse in any
event ...." (internal citation omitted); Reynolds v. Giuliani, 506 F.3d 183, 197 (2d Cir. 2007)
("[I]f the evidence before us admits only a single resolution of the controling issue, remanding
the case serves no useful purpose.'); In re Platinum and Palladium Antitrust Litig.,

the factfinder chooses between plausible alternatives, but differentiating plausible from

Turning to the lead respondent's application for protection under the CAT, we agree with the
Immigration Judge that the respondent has not demonstrated that he will more likely than not be
tortured in Ecuador by, at the instigation of, or with the consent or acquiescence ofa public official
(IJ at 11-12). See 8 C.F.R. §§ 1208.16(c), 1208.18(a). Although the respondent suffered some
physical harm by gang members in Ecuador in the past, he did not require medical treatment and
did not suffer any serious injuries (IJ at 11 n.4; Tr. at 40-41, 46, 48). See Ayv. Holder, 743 F.3d
317, 322 (2d Cir. 2014) (holding that a respondent who was *kicked, punched, and slapped after
being arrested by ... security forces," but did not require medical treatment did not suffer torture).
Overall, the respondent has not demonstrated .that his past harm was suficiently severe to rise to
the level of torture. See 8 C.F.R. § 1208.18(a)(2) (describing torture as an "extreme form of cruel
and inhuman treatment [that] does not include lesser forms of cruel. inhuman or degrading
treatment or punishment."); see also Garcia-Aranda v. Garland, 53 F.4th 752, 759 (2d Cir. 2022)
(describing torture as an "act by which severe pain or suffering, whether physical or mental, is
intentionally inflicted" (quoting 8 C.F.R. § 1208.18(a)(1))

The Immigration Judge also did not clearly err in finding that the respondent could avoid
torture by relocating within Ecuador (IJ at 11-12).. See Manning v. Barr, 954 F.3d 477, 488
(2d Cir. 2020) (holding that although a respondent does not have the burden to demonstrate that
relocation is not possible, evidence that the respondent could relocate to avoid torture is a factor
to consider). The harm the respondent suffered in the past all took place in the city of Cuenca
while he was a security guard (Tr. at 55-56). When asked why he did not move to the countryside
following the harm he suffered in Cuenca, the respondent cited financial limitations and lack of
family support there (Tr. at 49). He did not claim that he would be tortured in the countryside.
Although the respondent testified that there is insecurity everywhere in Ecuador and that there are
individuals who steal cattle in the countryside, the record does not demonstrate that the respondent
would more likely than not suffer torture by these or othier imdividuals if he were to relocate outside
of Cuenca (Tr. at 50-51, 53). See Zelaya-Moreno, 989 F.3d at 205 (holding that generalized
statements about the pervasiveness of gangs and gang violence were insufficient to demonstrate
that the respondent could not internally relocate to avoid harm)..

withholding ofremoval, the CAT reguations do not contain such a requirement and the respondent

the reasonableness of the relocation in the context of CAT protect.3 Compare 8 C.F.R.

The respondents also argue that the Immigration Judge did not sufficiently consider all the
evidence in the record in concluding that the lead respondent did not demonstrate government
acquiescence (IJ at 11-12; Respondents' Br. at 18-19). "Acquiescence is satisfied where the
govemment actor 'know[s] of or remain[s] willfully blind to an act and thereafter breach[es] their
legal responsibility to prevent it." Garcia-Aranda, 53 F.4th at 759 (quoting Khouzam v. Ashcroft
361 F.3d 161, 171 (2d Cir. 2004). The Immigration Judge found that the police responded when
called, conducted operations, and on at least one occasions, arrested the gang members who
harmed the respondent (IJ at 11; Tr. at 33-35,41-42, 47, 56). The Immigration Judge further found
based on the documentary evidence that the police have investigated the killing of security guards
in the past (IJ at 12; Exh. 4, Tab L). That the police were slow to respond does not demonstrate
acquiescence, as the documentary evidence reports that Ecuadoran police have limited resources
(Tr. at 33, 42, 56; Exh. 4, Tab M at 83, 91).
We are unpersuaded by the respondents' argument on appeal that the Immigration Judge failed
to consider evidence of govemment corruption in Ecuador (Respondents' Br. at 18-19). The
record contains some evidence of government corruption, including the respondent's testimony
that the gang members were released shortly after their arrest and that the gang members claimed
to have bribed the police (Tr. at 34-35, 49; Exh. 4, Tab M). This evidence, however, is insufficient
to satisfy the respondent's burden of proving that a public official will acquiesce to his torture.

The Department of State Human Rights Report indicates that impunity in the security forces is not
a significant problem in Ecuador and reports that the government took steps to implement the laws
against corruption effectively (Exh. 4, Tab M at 50, 66). While the documentary evidence reports
low conviction rates for major crimes (Exh. 4, Tab M at 91), we are not persuaded by the
respondents' argument on appeal that the Ecuadoran government is complicit in torture
(Respondents' Br. at 18-19). See Zaldana Menijar v. Lynch, 812 F.3d 491, 502 (6th Cir. 2015)
(holding that a government's inability to control gangs does not constitute acquiescence); see also
Scarlett v. Barr, 957 F.3d 316, 335 (2d Cir. 2020) (leaving to the agency the questions of how an
mability to control affects government acquiescence to torture). We agree with the Immigration
Judge that the respondent has not satisfied his burden of proofthat a public official will acquiesce.
to his torture in Ecuador (IJ at 11-12). See 8 C.F.R. s.1208.16(c)(2), 1208.18(a)(1), (7). Based
on the foregoing, we affirm the Immigration Judge's denial of the respondent's application for
CAT protection. The respondents' appeal will be dismissed.
In their appellate brief, the respondents seek remand to submit additional country conditions
evidence. Contrary to the respondents' argument, the new evidence does not demonstrate that
country conditions in Ecuador have materially changed since the respondents' proceedings before
the Immigration Judge (Respondents' Br. at 21-22). The documentary evidence discusses an
imcrease in.violence caused by organized crime since approximately 2021,prior to the respondents
January 2022 hearing before the Immigration Judge (Respondents' Br., Tab A at 24-25, 33-34,
52). Additionally, because the respondents have not demonstrated that they are members of a
cognzable particular social group, the new evidence of violence in Ecuador would not likely.
change the result of their applications for asylum and the lead respondent's application for
withholding of removal. See Matter of Coelho, 20 I&N Dec.. 464, 473 (holding that the Board wil
not consider a discretionary grant of a motion to remand unless the new evidence offered would
likely change the result of a case). Moreover, while the new evidence discusses the Ecuadoran
government's difficulty 'in combatting organzed crime and a general lack of confidence in the
government, it also discusses the concrete steps the government is taking to try to combat the
violence (Respondents' Br., Tab A at 25, 34-35, 47, 59). Thus, we are not persuaded that the new
evidence combined with the evidence previously before the Immigration Judge would likely
change the result of the respondent's application for CAT protection. See id. The motion to
remand will be denied. Accordingly, the following orders will be entered..
